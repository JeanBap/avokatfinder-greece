Golden Visa Greece

Greece's Golden Visa programme grants a five-year renewable residency permit to non-EU nationals who invest at least EUR 250,000 (rising to EUR 800,000 in key areas from 2024) in Greek real estate, making it one of Europe's most sought-after investor visas.

Greece's Golden Visa programme, introduced in 2013, offers a five-year renewable residency permit (and a path to citizenship after seven years) to non-EU nationals who make a qualifying real estate investment. The minimum investment was EUR 250,000 at launch, but a 2023 reform split the country into zones: properties in Attica, Thessaloniki, Mykonos, Santorini, and other high-demand islands now require EUR 800,000, while the EUR 250,000 threshold remains in less-pressured areas.

The visa covers the main applicant plus immediate family (spouse, children under 21, and parents). Crucially, the holder does not need to reside in Greece for any minimum period to maintain the permit — it is a residency-by-investment, not an active residency requirement. This distinguishes it from many other EU residency schemes.

From 2024, purely residential property purchases no longer qualify for the EUR 250,000 threshold in the affected zones regardless of location — the investment must be in commercial property converted to residential, heritage buildings, or qualifying tourist accommodation. The EUR 800,000 threshold applies to all residential property in the high-demand zones.

The Golden Visa process involves: selecting and completing the purchase (the property must be fully paid before applying), obtaining a Greek tax number (AFM), opening a Greek bank account, and submitting the application to the Migration Department. Processing typically takes three to six months.

Tax implications in the investor's home country vary widely. The visa itself is not taxable, but rental income from the Greek property and eventual capital gains are subject to Greek tax and potentially the home country's tax — specialist cross-border advice is essential.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Purchasing a EUR 250,000 property in an area that was reclassified to EUR 800,000 after announcement but before your completion will not qualify. The threshold applicable is determined by the date of the purchase deed, not the date of application.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Engage a Greek lawyer who specialises exclusively in Golden Visa applications — not just property transactions. The immigration and property legal processes must run in parallel, and specialist knowledge of both is required.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I rent out my Golden Visa property?

Yes. There are no restrictions on renting out a Golden Visa property, provided you comply with Greek short-term let registration rules. Rental income is subject to Greek income tax.

Does the Golden Visa lead to Greek citizenship?

After seven years of legal residency (not seven years of holding the visa — you must register as a resident), you may apply for naturalisation. The criteria include demonstrated ties to Greece and basic Greek language.

What happens if I sell the property?

The Golden Visa lapses unless you reinvest the proceeds in another qualifying property before or simultaneously with the sale. Holding the proceeds in cash does not maintain the visa.
