Δήλωση Ε9 (E9 Declaration)

Greek: Δήλωση Ε9 — Greek Property Register Declaration

The mandatory Greek tax authority declaration of all owned immovable property, filed via the ENFIA online system. Every property owner in Greece must have an up-to-date E9, which feeds into the ENFIA (property tax) calculation.

TL;DRThe mandatory Greek tax authority declaration of all owned immovable property, filed via the ENFIA online system. Every property owner in Greece must have an up-to-date E9, which feeds into the ENFIA (property tax) calculation.

The Δήλωση Ε9 (E9 declaration) is the mandatory online declaration filed with the Greek tax authority (Ανεξάρτητη Αρχή Δημοσίων Εσόδων — AADE) listing all immovable property owned by a taxpayer in Greece. It is the foundational document for the Greek property tax system — the ENFIA (Ενιαίος Φόρος Ιδιοκτησίας Ακινήτων) is calculated directly from the data declared in the E9. Every Greek property owner, resident or non-resident, must file and maintain an accurate E9.

The E9 must be updated whenever there is a change in your property portfolio: purchase of a new property, sale of an existing property, inheritance, gift, or any change in ownership percentage. Updates must be filed within the statutory deadline — typically within 30 days of the change (e.g. within 30 days of the symolaio date). The E9 is filed electronically through the AADE's myTAXIS portal (mytaxis.aade.gr).

For expat buyers purchasing Greek property, the E9 obligation begins immediately upon completion. You must obtain a Greek AFM (tax identification number) before you can access the myTAXIS portal, and you must file the E9 declaring your new property within the deadline. Your Greek accountant or lawyer typically handles this as part of the post-completion process, but the obligation is yours as the new owner.

The information declared in the E9 feeds directly into the ENFIA calculation: the system uses the property's location, size, construction year, floor level (for apartments), and zone value to compute the annual property tax. Inaccurate E9 declarations — understating size, misclassifying property type, or omitting properties — are subject to penalties and can trigger tax audits.

When purchasing Greek property, you should receive from the seller a copy of their current E9 showing the property you are buying. Cross-reference the declared details against the building permit (oikodomiki adeio), the topografiko (topographic survey), and the Ktimatologio entry. Discrepancies may indicate undeclared extensions or irregularities that could affect your future tax position.

⚠ Common MistakeMissing the 30-day E9 update deadline after purchasing Greek property. Late E9 filings incur penalties, and an outdated E9 means your ENFIA bill will not correctly reflect your ownership — either continuing to charge the previous owner (creating administrative confusion) or failing to charge you (creating a tax debt that accumulates with interest).

Bundle this with the AFM registration if you do not yet have a Greek tax number. The E9 filing itself is straightforward once you have AFM access.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the ENFIA and how much is it?

ENFIA (Unified Property Ownership Tax) is Greece's annual property tax. The amount varies significantly based on location zone value, property size, and type. In central Athens and tourist islands, ENFIA on a 100m² apartment might range from €300 to €3,000+ annually. Rural properties typically pay much less. Your Greek accountant can calculate the expected ENFIA before purchase.

Do I need a Greek AFM to file an E9?

Yes — the AFM (Αριθμός Φορολογικού Μητρώου) is your Greek tax identification number and is required to access myTAXIS and file the E9. Non-residents who own Greek property must obtain an AFM from their local AADE tax office or via a Greek tax representative. This is typically done before or simultaneously with the symolaio signing.

What if the previous owner's E9 shows the property with different dimensions than the actual building?

This is common in Greece, where many properties have undeclared extensions or irregularities. Your obligation is to declare the property accurately from the date of your acquisition — not to inherit the previous owner's inaccuracies. However, if the property has undeclared works, this may create separate legal issues around building permits and habitability that your lawyer should investigate.
