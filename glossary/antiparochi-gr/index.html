Antiparochi (Exchange of Land for Apartments)

Antiparochi is a uniquely Greek property development arrangement where a landowner provides their plot to a developer in exchange for a share of the newly built apartments, without any cash changing hands.

Antiparochi (literally 'exchange in return') is a development model deeply embedded in Greek property history and still widely used today. Under an antiparochi agreement, a landowner transfers their plot to a developer who constructs a multi-storey apartment building on it. In return, the developer gives the landowner a pre-agreed percentage of the finished apartments — typically 25–40% depending on the location and plot value — while retaining the rest for sale.

This model emerged in post-war Greece as a way to develop urban areas rapidly without requiring capital from landowners. It remains prevalent in Athens and other cities where land values are high relative to construction costs. Many Greek families acquired their apartments through antiparochi agreements made by their parents or grandparents.

For buyers, the key issue is understanding the chain of title created by antiparochi. The developer retains title to their share of apartments and sells them to buyers. The landowner retains title to their share. These two sets of apartments within the same building have different ownership chains that buyers must verify in the Ktimatologio.

Disputes can arise in antiparochi buildings when the original agreement's terms are contested — particularly around which specific apartments the landowner was promised, or when construction deviated from the agreed plan. Buyers should check that the specific apartment they are purchasing was formally allocated to the selling party (developer or landowner) in the original antiparochi agreement.

Antiparochi transactions are not subject to transfer tax in the same way as standard property sales — the landowner's exchange is typically subject to capital gains rules rather than FMA. Tax structuring in antiparochi arrangements is complex and specialist advice is essential.

Common Mistake

Watch out: Assuming all apartments in a Greek building have identical title chains is wrong when antiparochi was involved. Some units may be traced to the original landowner, others to the developer — and their legal histories differ. Always verify the specific chain for your unit.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Ask your lawyer to obtain and review the original antiparochi agreement (symfonitiko antiparochis) for the building before exchange. This document defines which units went to whom and is the root of title for the whole development.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is antiparochi still used for new developments?

Yes, though less universally than in the mid-20th century. In prime Athens locations where land values are very high, landowners often prefer cash sales. In secondary cities and suburbs, antiparochi remains common.

Can I buy an antiparochi apartment if I am a foreigner?

Yes. There are no nationality restrictions on purchasing an antiparochi apartment. The due diligence process is the same — verify title chain, KAEK, and encumbrances in the Ktimatologio.

What percentage of apartments does the landowner typically receive?

The landowner's share (pososto) typically ranges from 25–40% of the total floor area, negotiated based on the plot's value, location, and planning permissions. The exact split is set out in the antiparochi agreement.
