Υποθήκη (Ypothiki)

Greek: Υποθήκη — Greek Mortgage / Property Charge

A Greek mortgage charge registered at the Land Registry (Κτηματολόγιο or Υποθηκοφυλακείο) as security for a loan. Must be satisfied and cancelled before or at property sale completion.

TL;DRA Greek mortgage charge registered at the Land Registry (Κτηματολόγιο or Υποθηκοφυλακείο) as security for a loan. Must be satisfied and cancelled before or at property sale completion.

An ypothiki (υποθήκη) in Greek law is a real security right over immovable property, giving a creditor (typically a bank) the right to priority payment from sale proceeds if the debtor defaults. It is created by notarial deed and registered at the competent land registry — either the Ktimatologio (Κτηματολόγιο, the modern national cadastral registry) or the older Ypothikofylakeio (Υποθηκοφυλακείο, the traditional mortgage registry), depending on which area of Greece the property is in.

Greek mortgage law distinguishes between the ypothiki (voluntary mortgage created by agreement) and the prosiminosi (προσημείωση ypothikis — a preliminary notation that serves as provisional security pending the court's confirmation as a full mortgage). Banks frequently use the prosiminosi as it is faster to register and can be converted to a full ypothiki after judicial confirmation. Both appear in land registry searches.

Before any Greek property sale can complete, the notarios (notary) conducts a dikaiotitlos (title search) at the competent registry. All registered ypothikes must be discharged from the sale proceeds before the seller receives any net amount. An unresolved ypothiki blocks the transfer — the registry will refuse to register a new owner without confirmation that all charges are cleared.

Greek banks have accelerated their ypothiki enforcement processes since the 2010s financial crisis. Non-performing mortgage borrowers face foreclosure proceedings (pleistrikiasmos) that can result in forced sale of the property — a reality that has created a significant market of auctioned (kataschetheisa) properties available to investors. These properties carry their own legal complexity and require specialist legal advice.

For expat buyers purchasing Greek property with Greek mortgage financing, the ypothiki is created simultaneously with the purchase deed (symolaio). Non-resident buyers may face stricter lending conditions from Greek banks — higher deposit requirements, additional income documentation, and limits on LTV (loan-to-value ratio). Some buyers use foreign-bank financing or equity release from other property, avoiding the Greek ypothiki entirely.

⚠ Common MistakePurchasing Greek property without confirming that all registered ypothikes (including prosimioseis) are discharged at or before the symolaio signing. An undisclosed or overlooked ypothiki can leave you owning a property with a bank's priority claim over it — nullifying much of your investment.
💡 Expert TipAsk your Greek lawyer to obtain a full title search (erevna tou Ktimatologiou kai tou Ypothikofylakeiou) at least two weeks before the scheduled symolaio signing. This search reveals all registered charges, encumbrances, and pending court proceedings. Pay for the search yourself to ensure it is independent of the seller's interests.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a Ktimatologio search and a Ypothikofylakeio search?

The Ktimatologio is the modern national cadastral and property register being rolled out across Greece — it records both ownership and encumbrances. The Ypothikofylakeio is the traditional registry that still operates in areas not yet transferred to the Ktimatologio. In some areas, both must be searched. Your Greek lawyer will know which applies.

Can a Greek bank hold an ypothiki over property I own in another country?

No — Greek ypothiki law covers only property located in Greece. For international lending, a Greek bank may additionally require security over foreign assets under the laws of the relevant country, but this is separate from the Greek ypothiki.

How long does it take to discharge a Greek ypothiki?

Once the loan is repaid, the bank issues a discharge document (praxis arsis ypothikis). This must be registered at the competent registry by a notarios — a process that typically takes 1–4 weeks. If you are selling, coordinate the discharge timing with your notarios well in advance of the symolaio date.
