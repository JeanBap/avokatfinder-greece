Synidioktisia (Co-ownership)

Synidioktisia is Greek co-ownership of property, where two or more persons hold undivided shares (adiaireti meridi) in a single property. It arises frequently through inheritance and creates specific challenges for sale and management.

Synidioktisia (co-ownership) arises when two or more persons own shares in the same property — typically through inheritance (klirodotia), but also through joint purchase. Each co-owner holds an adiaireti meridi (undivided share) — a percentage of the whole property, not a specific physical portion. No co-owner can point to a particular room or section of the property and claim it as exclusively theirs without a formal partition (dianomi).

Greek inheritance law (forced heirship rules) frequently creates synidioktisia in older properties. A property originally owned by one person may, after two or three generations of intestate succession, be co-owned by a dozen or more descendants in fractional shares. This 'fragmented title' is one of the most common obstacles encountered in Greek real estate transactions.

All co-owners must consent to a sale or mortgage of the whole property. A single dissenting co-owner — even one holding a 1/10 share — can block a transaction. The only remedy is a court-ordered partition (dikaiosyni dianomi) or a forced auction, both of which are slow and expensive.

Buyers are strongly advised to identify all co-owners of a Greek property before making any offer. This requires not just a Ktimatologio extract but often a genealogical search (ereunia agoronomikon egrafon) and death and birth certificate searches to establish the current ownership chain after deaths and successions.

When one co-owner wants to sell their specific share, they must first offer it to the other co-owners under the right of pre-emption (protimisi). Only if all co-owners decline can the share be sold to an outside buyer.

Key Facts

NatureUndivided fractional shares — no physical division
Common originInheritance (intestate succession)
Sale requirementAll co-owners must consent
Pre-emption rightOther co-owners must be offered the share first
ResolutionPartition (dianomi) or court-ordered auction

Common Mistake

Watch out: Buying a fractional share of a Greek property with the intention of developing or selling the whole is extremely high-risk unless you have agreements in place with all other co-owners. A single hold-out can paralyse the asset indefinitely.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: Before making any offer on a Greek property, instruct your lawyer to confirm the exact ownership structure from the Ktimatologio or Ypothikofilakia. Discovering unexpected co-owners after exchange is a major problem.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I force a partition if I am a co-owner?

Yes. Greek law allows any co-owner to demand partition through the courts (agogio dianomis). The court may order a physical division of the property if divisible, or a forced sale with proceeds distributed proportionally.

What is the protimisi right?

The statutory right of first refusal that other co-owners hold when one co-owner wishes to sell their share to an external buyer. The selling co-owner must first formally offer the share to all other co-owners at the same price. Only after they decline (or fail to respond within 2 months) can the share be sold externally.

Can I buy a property with synidioktisia?

Yes, provided all co-owners consent and sign the notarial deed. The risk is ensuring that all shares are accounted for — hidden co-owners (e.g. undocumented heirs) can challenge the transaction after completion.
