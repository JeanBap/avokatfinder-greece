Greece Legal Term

Τοπογραφικό (Topografiko)

Greek: Τοπογραφικό Διάγραμμα — Topographic Survey

A certified topographic survey plan of a Greek property, prepared by a licensed civil engineer (topografos). Required for building permits, land registry registration, and many property sales. Essential due diligence for rural plots.

TL;DRA certified topographic survey plan of a Greek property, prepared by a licensed civil engineer (topografos). Required for building permits, land registry registration, and many property sales. Essential due diligence for rural plots.

A topografiko (τοπογραφικό διάγραμμα — topographic survey plan) is an accurate scaled drawing of a plot of land prepared by a licensed civil engineer (μηχανικός/τοπογράφος). It shows the plot's boundaries, dimensions, total area, coordinates, relation to neighbouring properties and roads, and any buildings on the land. The topografiko must comply with requirements set by the Greek Technical Chamber (Τεχνικό Επιμελητήριο Ελλάδος — TEE).

A topografiko with a forestry declaration (βεβαίωση μη δάσους — not-forest certificate or δασική αναφορά) is required for building permits, Ktimatologio registration in some areas, and many Greek property sales — particularly for land outside urban zones. The not-forest certificate from the Forestry Service (Δασαρχείο) confirms the plot is not classified as forest land, which would prohibit construction and affect the right to sell freely.

For rural plots and land outside city planning zones (εκτός σχεδίου), the topografiko and not-forest certificate are non-negotiable due diligence items. Greek law imposes strict minimum size requirements for building on unplanned land (typically 4,000m² or more depending on the area and region), and forest land cannot be built on at all. Misclassified land — sold as buildable when it is legally forest — is a serious and not uncommon problem in Greek property.

The cost of a topografiko varies by plot complexity and location — typically €400–1,500 for residential plots. Preparation takes 2–6 weeks. The engineer must physically survey the plot, check satellite imagery, consult local authority records, and in some cases appear before the Ktimatologio to resolve boundary disputes. For properties already registered in the Ktimatologio, existing cadastral maps may reduce the need for a new full survey, but a verifying topografiko is still advisable before any purchase.

Boundary disputes (αμφισβητήσεις ορίων) between neighbouring landowners are endemic in Greek rural areas, where historical informal divisions — marked by stone walls, trees, or verbal agreements — do not match official records. A good topografiko surfaces these issues before purchase rather than after. Never buy Greek land or a rural property without a current, engineer-certified topografiko in hand.

⚠ Common MistakePurchasing a Greek rural plot on the basis of the seller's stated area without an independent topografiko. Greek plots often differ from stated sizes by 10–30% — sometimes more — due to historical informal boundary agreements. An independent survey before purchase, not after, is the only way to confirm what you are actually buying.
💡 Expert TipIf the seller has an existing topografiko, have your own engineer independently verify it — do not simply rely on the seller's document. Also separately request the not-forest certificate (βεβαίωση μη δάσους) from the local Forestry Service — a fundamental check that confirms the land is not classified as forest, which would severely restrict sale and development rights.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a topografiko required for urban apartment purchases in Greece?

For most urban apartment purchases, a full topografiko is not required as the building is already registered and the Ktimatologio provides cadastral information. However, for ground-floor apartments with private gardens or terraces, or for purchases involving parking spaces or storage rooms with unclear cadastral status, an engineer's verification is advisable.

What is the difference between a topografiko and a ktimatologiko diagrama?

A topografiko is prepared by a private engineer for a specific purpose (building permit, transaction). A ktimatologiko diagrama is the official cadastral map produced by the national Ktimatologio. Both show the plot, but the ktimatologiko diagrama is the authoritative official record, while the topografiko may be more detailed and current for the specific site conditions.

Can boundary disputes be resolved before a sale?

Yes — your civil engineer can negotiate boundary adjustments with neighbouring landowners, formalised by a mutual declaration. More serious disputes require court proceedings (ktimatologiki diki) before the Ktimatologio. Purchasing a plot with an unresolved boundary dispute is risky — the dispute transfers to you and litigation can take years.
