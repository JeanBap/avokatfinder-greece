Φόρος Κληρονομιάς (Foros Klirodosias)

Greek: Φόρος Κληρονομιάς — Greek Inheritance Tax

Greek inheritance tax levied on property and assets inherited upon death. Rates depend on the relationship between deceased and heir — close family (Category A) pay 1–10%; more distant relatives pay up to 40%.

TL;DRGreek inheritance tax levied on property and assets inherited upon death. Rates depend on the relationship between deceased and heir — close family (Category A) pay 1–10%; more distant relatives pay up to 40%.

Foros Klirodosias (φόρος κληρονομιάς — inheritance tax) is levied on assets transferred upon death in Greece. Greek inheritance tax applies to: Greek real estate regardless of where the deceased lived; and the worldwide assets of Greek residents. For non-Greek residents who own Greek property, inheritance tax is limited to the Greek-situated assets.

Greek inheritance tax uses three categories based on relationship to the deceased. Category A (spouse, children, grandchildren, parents) benefits from a substantial tax-free allowance of €150,000 per heir, with rates of 1–10% on the excess. Category B (siblings, grandparents, aunts, uncles, nieces, nephews) has a €30,000 allowance and rates of 5–20%. Category C (all others, including unmarried partners and friends) has a €6,000 allowance and rates of 20–40%.

Unmarried partners — even in long-term cohabiting relationships — fall into Category C under current Greek law, unless they have registered a civil partnership (σύμφωνο συμβίωσης — symfouno symviosis). This is a critical planning point for expat couples in Greece or owning Greek property: an unregistered partner inheriting a Greek property worth €500,000 would pay inheritance tax of approximately €160,000, while a spouse would pay approximately €17,500.

Greek inheritance tax on real estate is calculated on the official objective value (αντικειμενική αξία — antikeimenike axia) of the property — typically significantly below market value — rather than on the market price. This makes the effective tax burden lower than nominal rates suggest. However, the objective value has been rising in recent years, particularly in Athens, Thessaloniki, and the islands.

Practical process: heirs must file a declaration of acceptance (αποδοχή κληρονομιάς) within a statutory deadline (typically 4 months if the deceased was in Greece, 12 months if abroad) and file the inheritance tax declaration with the tax authority (AADE). Greek property transfers to heirs require a court declaration of heirship (κληρονομητήριο) or a formal notarial deed, and must be registered in the Ktimatologio in the heir's name before the property can be sold.

⚠ Common MistakeLeaving Greek property jointly or solely to an unmarried partner without registering a civil partnership (symfouno symviosis) first. Without registration, your partner falls into Category C — facing tax rates of up to 40% on the property's value above €6,000. This is an entirely avoidable planning failure.
💡 Expert TipIf you own Greek property and have family members who might inherit it, obtain a Greek inheritance tax calculation from a Greek lawyer now — not after you die. Understanding the future tax liability allows you to structure ownership (e.g. gifting during your lifetime, setting up a civil partnership, or purchasing through a structure) to legitimately minimise the burden on your heirs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Greek inheritance tax apply to non-Greek citizens inheriting Greek property?

Yes — Greek inheritance tax applies to Greek real estate regardless of the nationality of the heir or the deceased. A British heir inheriting a Greek island villa from a British relative who never lived in Greece still owes Greek foros klirodosias on the property's objective value.

Can I avoid Greek inheritance tax by transferring property to my children during my lifetime?

Lifetime gifts (δωρεά) are subject to Greek gift tax (foros doreas) using the same three-category structure and rates as inheritance tax — so the rates are the same. However, gifting allows you to use the tax-free allowances during your lifetime, potentially making multiple gifts over time to spread the liability. Consult a Greek tax lawyer on the optimal strategy for your circumstances.

What happens if I do not accept a Greek inheritance?

You may renounce (αποποίηση) a Greek inheritance within 4 months of learning of it (12 months if abroad). This is done by formal declaration before a notarios or court. Renunciation is irrevocable and results in the inheritance passing to the next heir in line. Partial acceptance is not permitted — you either accept the whole inheritance or renounce the whole.
