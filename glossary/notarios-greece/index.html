Greece Legal Term

Συμβολαιογράφος (Symvolaiorafos)

Greek: Συμβολαιογράφος — Greek Notary

A Greek civil law notary (symvolaiorafos), a state-appointed official who drafts, authenticates, and archives all notarial deeds including property sale contracts (symolaia). Mandatory for all Greek property sales.

TL;DRA Greek civil law notary (symvolaiorafos), a state-appointed official who drafts, authenticates, and archives all notarial deeds including property sale contracts (symolaia). Mandatory for all Greek property sales.

The symvolaiorafos (συμβολαιογράφος — Greek notary) is a state-appointed legal professional responsible for drafting and authenticating legally binding documents — primarily property deeds (symolaio), company formation documents, wills, and powers of attorney. The symvolaiorafos is a public official operating within a regulated framework set by the Greek Notarial Code (Κώδικας Συμβολαιογράφων). Their involvement is mandatory for all Greek property sales — there is no alternative mechanism for transferring real property in Greece.

At a Greek property transaction, the symvolaiorafos performs several critical functions: conducting a title search (εκτύπωση ιστορικού ιδιοκτησίας) at the competent land registry to confirm the seller's title and identify encumbrances; drafting the symolaio (sale deed) incorporating all agreed terms; reading the document aloud to all parties; authenticating signatures; and filing the deed with the registry immediately after signing.

Greek notarial fees are regulated by law and calculated as a percentage of the property's objective value (αντικειμενική αξία) — not the actual sale price. This means fees are often lower than in France or Italy where they are based on actual prices. For a property with an objective value of €200,000, total notarial fees (including all stamps and disbursements) typically range from €1,500 to €3,500.

Unlike in some European countries where the notary acts as escrow agent, Greek symvolaiorafoi do not routinely hold purchase funds in escrow. Payment of the purchase price typically occurs at the signing table by bank-certified cheque (trapeziki epitagi), with funds exchanged simultaneously with the signed deed. This makes the Greek completion process more reliant on coordinated bank-to-bank transfers and buyer preparation than the escrow systems of France or Germany.

For foreign buyers who do not speak Greek, the symvolaiorafos must read the entire deed in Greek — this is a legal requirement. A sworn interpreter (διερμηνέας) should be present if you do not understand Greek, or you must grant a procuration (πληρεξούσιο) to a Greek-speaking representative. Never sign a Greek property deed without fully understanding its contents — either personally in Greek or through a qualified interpreter.

⚠ Common MistakeUsing the seller's recommended symvolaiorafos without engaging your own Greek lawyer to review the symolaio draft first. The symvolaiorafos is neutral — they verify legal formality, not your commercial interests. A separate Greek lawyer (δικηγόρος) reviewing the title and the deed terms is essential protection for expat buyers.
💡 Expert TipRequest the draft symolaio from the symvolaiorafos at least one week before the signing date. This is your legal right. Have your Greek lawyer review it for: accuracy of the property description and cadastral references, any encumbrances not disclosed, permit status declarations, payment terms, and any unusual conditions. Catching problems at draft stage is far easier than unwinding a signed deed.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I choose any symvolaiorafos in Greece, or must I use one local to the property?

You may use any symvolaiorafos who is competent in the jurisdiction where the property is located. While there is no strict geographical restriction in principle, local symvolaiorafoi are familiar with the local land registry, urban planning office, and any regional quirks — making them the practical choice. Your Greek lawyer can recommend a trusted local notary.

What does the symvolaiorafos check before drafting the symolaio?

The symvolaiorafos obtains extracts from the competent land registry (Ktimatologio or Ypothikofylakeio) confirming ownership and encumbrances, a tax clearance certificate (ENFIA clearance) from the seller, and confirmation that the seller has no outstanding debts to the Greek tax authority. These checks, combined with the seller's permit declaration, form the baseline due diligence before proceeding.

If I discover a defect after the symolaio is signed, can I hold the symvolaiorafos liable?

Only if the symvolaiorafos failed to perform a check they were legally required to do (e.g. failed to search the land registry). They are not liable for defects they had no means to discover. Claims against a symvolaiorafos for professional negligence are possible but rare and require evidence of a specific duty of care that was breached.
