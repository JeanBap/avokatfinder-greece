Greece Legal Term

Οικοδομική Άδεια (Oikodomiki Adeio)

Greek: Οικοδομική Άδεια — Building Permit

Greek building permit authorising construction, extension, or significant renovation of a property. Issued by local Urban Planning Services. Any work without a permit creates an "arbitrary construction" (αυθαίρετο) — a widespread issue in Greek property.

TL;DRGreek building permit authorising construction, extension, or significant renovation of a property. Issued by local Urban Planning Services. Any work without a permit creates an "arbitrary construction" (αυθαίρετο) — a widespread issue in Greek property.

An oikodomiki adeio (οικοδομική άδεια — building permit) is the authorisation issued by the Greek Urban Planning Service (Υπηρεσία Δόμησης — YPOM) of the relevant municipality for construction, substantial extension, or significant structural renovation of a building. All new buildings and major works require a permit — unauthorised construction (αυθαίρετο κτίσμα — arbitrary building) is technically illegal, subject to demolition orders, and creates complications for sale, financing, and property registration.

Greece has a pervasive arbitrary building problem — it is estimated that a significant proportion of Greek buildings contain some element of unauthorised construction: an enclosed balcony, a converted attic, an unauthorised extra floor, a swimming pool without permits. Multiple amnesties (ρυθμίσεις αυθαιρέτων) have been issued since 2011, allowing owners to declare and temporarily regularise unauthorised works by paying a fine — not permanently legalising them, but suspending demolition orders and allowing sale.

When purchasing Greek property, your notarios must obtain a declaration from the seller confirming the property's building permit status and that any arbitrary elements have been declared and regularised under the relevant amnesty law. Your Greek engineer should independently verify the permit against the actual structure — checking whether the built area matches the permit, whether any additions were made without authorisation, and what amnesty declarations if any are on file.

The consequences of undeclared arbitrary construction are serious: the buyer inherits the violations; future sale becomes problematic; Greek lenders typically will not finance properties with undeclared arbitrary elements; and the authorities can in theory issue demolition orders — though enforcement is inconsistent. Post-purchase discovery of undisclosed arbitrary works gives the buyer grounds for rescission of the sale or price reduction (for vices cachés / hidden defects).

New buildings in Greece require an oikodomiki adeio before construction begins and a certificate of completion (τελικό πιστοποιητικό ελέγχου — tepiko pistopoiitiko elegchou) after completion. The completion certificate confirms the building matches the permit and is required for the issuance of a connection to public utilities (water, electricity) and for the building to be legally occupied.

⚠ Common MistakeAccepting a seller's verbal assurance that "everything is in order with the permits" without independent engineering verification. Greece's arbitrary building problem is extensive and normalised — "it's fine, everyone does it" is not a substitute for a written permit check by your own licensed engineer.

This €300–600 check often reveals discrepancies. Any unauthorised area must be regularised under the current amnesty (if eligible) or removed — costs and risks the seller should bear before completion.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get a Greek mortgage on a property with arbitrary construction?

Greek banks typically refuse to finance properties with undeclared arbitrary construction. Declared arbitrary elements that have been regularised under an amnesty may be acceptable to some lenders, subject to their individual policies. Always confirm the financing position with your bank's surveyor early in the process — do not assume a property with arbitrary elements will be mortgageable.

What is the current Greek amnesty for arbitrary buildings?

Greece has issued several successive amnesties (most recently Law 4685/2020 and its extensions). These allow owners to declare unauthorised works, pay a regularisation fine (based on the area, location zone, and type of violation), and suspend demolition orders. Regularisation does not make the works permanently legal — they remain "tolerated" rather than fully permitted — but it allows sale and removes the immediate demolition threat.

Who is liable for undeclared arbitrary works I discover after buying?

If the seller concealed known arbitrary works, this constitutes a hidden defect (κρυφό ελάττωμα) giving you the right to cancel the sale and recover your money, or to claim a price reduction. The seller must declare all known violations in the symolaio. Consult your Greek lawyer immediately if you discover undisclosed works post-completion.
