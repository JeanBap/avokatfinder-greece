Φόρος Υπεραξίας (Foros Yperastrofis)

Greek: Φόρος Υπεραξίας Ακινήτων — Greek Property Capital Gains Tax

Greek capital gains tax on property sales, currently suspended since 2014. The suspension continues to be extended annually. If reinstated, the rate would be 15% on gains from sales within 5 years of purchase.

TL;DRGreek capital gains tax on property sales, currently suspended since 2014. The suspension continues to be extended annually. If reinstated, the rate would be 15% on gains from sales within 5 years of purchase.

Foros yperastrofis (φόρος υπεραξίας ακινήτων — property capital gains tax) is a Greek tax on profits from real estate sales that was introduced in 2014 but immediately suspended pending implementation. The suspension has been renewed every year since, effectively meaning that Greece has had no capital gains tax on property since the relevant provisions of Law 4172/2013 were first enacted. As of 2026, the suspension continues.

The suspended tax, if ever reinstated, would apply at 15% on gains from sales of Greek property within five years of acquisition. Property held for more than five years would be exempt. The tax base would be the difference between the sale price and the acquisition cost (including purchase taxes and documented improvements), adjusted for inflation.

The practical consequence for property owners and investors is that Greek property sales currently incur no capital gains tax for the seller — a significant advantage compared to France (19–36.2%), Italy (26%), or Austria (30%). This CGT-free environment has been a factor attracting international real estate investment, particularly in Athens and the islands.

The Greek government has repeatedly postponed full implementation, citing concerns about the impact on the property market. While the CGT suspension is expected to continue in the near term, buyers who plan to sell within 5 years should be aware that a future reinstatement is possible — albeit politically difficult given the housing crisis context.

In the absence of Greek CGT, sellers still have other tax obligations: the transfer tax (Φόρος Μεταβίβασης Ακινήτων — FMA) is paid by the buyer (3.09% of the objective value); the seller must declare the disposal in their annual income tax return; and if the seller is a professional real estate dealer (not an individual), different tax rules apply. The FMA and VAT on new builds are entirely the buyer's costs, not related to CGT.

⚠ Common MistakePlanning a Greek property exit strategy assuming the CGT suspension is permanent. The Greek government can reinstate the 15% CGT at any time with limited notice. Any significant investment decision based on the assumption of permanent CGT exemption should be stress-tested against the scenario of reinstatement.
💡 Expert TipEven with no Greek CGT currently applicable, your home country may tax gains on your Greek property sale. UK, US, German, and most European residents must declare Greek property gains in their domestic tax returns and apply any applicable domestic CGT — with a credit for any tax paid in Greece (currently zero). Don't assume "no Greek CGT" means "no tax anywhere".

Frequently Asked Questions

Is there any other tax on selling Greek property besides the suspended CGT?

The seller pays no FMA (that is the buyer's cost). The seller's main obligation is to declare the sale in their Greek income tax return and pay any other applicable taxes (e.g. if they are a professional dealer). For most private individual sellers, the suspended CGT means no significant Greek tax on the gain from a property sale.

Does the CGT suspension apply to non-resident sellers?

Yes — the suspension applies regardless of the seller's residency. Non-residents selling Greek property currently have no Greek CGT liability on the gain. However, they should check their home country's tax treatment of the gain, as most double tax treaties assign taxing rights over immovable property gains to the country where the property is located — meaning Greek CGT would be the primary tax if reinstated.

What happens to the objective value (antikeimenike axia) when calculating gains?

If the CGT is reinstated, the gain would be calculated using either the declared sale price or the objective value, whichever is higher — the same anti-under-declaration mechanism used for FMA. Gains would be calculated from the documented acquisition cost at the time of original purchase.
