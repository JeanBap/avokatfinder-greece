ENFIA (Unified Property Tax)

ENFIA (Ενιαίος Φόρος Ιδιοκτησίας Ακινήτων), the Greek unified property tax on all real estate owned by individuals and entities.

Definition

ENFIA, or Ενιαίος Φόρος Ιδιοκτησίας Ακινήτων (Eniaios Foros Idioktisias Akiniton), is Greece's unified property tax imposed on all real estate properties, including residential homes, commercial buildings, agricultural land, and undeveloped land. Introduced in 2013 to consolidate multiple property-related taxes, ENFIA is administered by the Greek Tax Authority (AADE) and is a primary revenue source for the Greek government. The tax applies to all property owners, including Greek citizens, EU residents, and non-EU property owners. Property subject to ENFIA ranges from modest apartments to commercial complexes and includes inherited properties, regardless of whether they generate income.

ENFIA tax rates are progressive and depend on the property's location and estimated value. Properties are assessed based on zoning classification, dimensions, year of construction, and market value estimates. Residential properties are typically taxed at lower rates than commercial or agricultural properties. Urban properties (located within city limits) are valued differently from rural properties. Special exemptions or reduced rates may apply to certain property types, such as primary residences with low assessed values or agricultural land in less-developed regions. The tax authority uses standardised valuation models to estimate property values for tax purposes, which may differ significantly from actual market prices.

Annual ENFIA payments are calculated by the Greek Tax Authority and property owners typically receive tax notices (δήλωση φόρου) indicating the amount due. Payments are usually made annually, though the tax authority may issue notices for multiple years if properties were previously untaxed or unregistered. For expatriates and foreign property owners, ENFIA is a non-negotiable obligation. The tax authority requires that properties be properly registered in the Land Registry (Κτηματολόγιο) and that owners keep current contact information on file.

Non-payment of ENFIA results in significant penalties, interest charges, and potential asset freezes. The tax authority has broad enforcement powers and can pursue collection through garnishment of bank accounts, seizure of other assets, or denial of permits for property transactions. Unpaid ENFIA can also prevent the sale or refinancing of property, as the tax debt must be settled before title can be transferred. Foreign property owners are subject to the same enforcement mechanisms as Greek citizens, making payment compliance essential.

Property owners should maintain accurate records and respond promptly to any tax authority communications regarding ENFIA. If you believe your ENFIA assessment is incorrect—for example, if the estimated property value is significantly higher than market value—you can file an objection with the tax authorities within specified timeframes. Documentation such as recent property appraisals, comparable property sales, or structural damage assessments can support objections. Consulting a Greek tax accountant or lawyer familiar with property taxation can help optimise your ENFIA liability and ensure compliance.

Key Facts

  • ENFIA is an annual property tax on all real estate, regardless of whether the property generates income
  • Tax rates are progressive and based on property location, zoning classification, and estimated value
  • The Greek Tax Authority (AADE) calculates and issues ENFIA assessments, with payments typically due annually
  • Foreign property owners are subject to ENFIA and have the same payment obligations as Greek citizens
  • Non-payment results in penalties, interest, asset freezes, and restrictions on property sales or refinancing

Common Mistake

Expatriates sometimes assume that owning property abroad exempts them from Greek ENFIA, or they delay registering property in the Land Registry, not realising that unregistered property is still subject to taxation. Another error is ignoring ENFIA notices or assuming assessments can be postponed indefinitely. Accumulated unpaid ENFIA creates mounting interest and penalties, and can result in forced asset sales to satisfy tax debts.

Expert Tip

If you own property in Greece, ensure it is properly registered in the Land Registry (Κτηματολόγιο) and that your contact information is current with the tax authority. Review your annual ENFIA assessment carefully and object if you believe the valuation is incorrect. Maintain documentation of property improvements, maintenance costs, and comparable property values, as these can support objections to inflated assessments. Consider engaging a Greek tax specialist to review your ENFIA liability and identify any available exemptions or reductions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is ENFIA calculated the same for all property types?

No. ENFIA rates and assessments vary significantly based on property type, location, and zoning. Residential properties are typically taxed at lower rates than commercial or agricultural properties. Urban properties face higher valuations than rural properties. The tax authority applies different assessment methodologies depending on property classification.

Can I request a reduction in my ENFIA assessment?

Yes. If you believe your ENFIA assessment is incorrect, you can file an objection with the tax authority within specified timeframes. Objections should include supporting documentation such as property appraisals, evidence of structural damage, or comparable property sales. Successful objections can result in reduced assessments and lower tax bills.

What happens if I don't pay ENFIA?

Non-payment results in penalties and interest charges that accumulate over time. The tax authority can freeze bank accounts, seize assets, or deny permits for property transactions. Unpaid ENFIA must be settled before property can be sold or refinanced. Persistent non-payment can trigger criminal tax evasion investigations.
