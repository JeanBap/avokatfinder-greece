1. Introduction: Greece's Appeal and the Administrative Framework

Greece has emerged as one of Europe's most attractive destinations for digital nomads, retirees, and entrepreneurs seeking Mediterranean lifestyle, ancient cultural heritage, and increasingly favorable tax conditions. The introduction of the digital nomad visa in 2022 and special tax regimes for new residents have created unprecedented opportunities for foreign nationals to establish residency in Greece. Athens, with its thriving expat community and cosmopolitan infrastructure, competes with London and Berlin for international talent. The islands—Crete, Rhodes, Santorini, and others—attract those seeking tranquility, natural beauty, and lower cost of living. Yet Greece's administrative system can be challenging for newcomers unfamiliar with Greek bureaucratic procedures.

The Greek government has implemented significant reforms in recent years to streamline residency and business registration processes, though bureaucracy remains complex. Unlike Italy's formal, document-heavy system, Greece's system is often more flexible but less transparent—procedures vary by municipality and government office, and documentation requirements can be unclear until you speak directly with the relevant authority. Successfully navigating Greek administrative processes requires understanding both formal procedures and practical workarounds.

This guide walks you through every essential document and procedure for Greek residency, from pre-departure preparation through your first months in Greece. We cover residence permits (with dedicated sections for EU citizens, non-EU citizens, and digital nomads), AFM tax number registration, AMKA social security registration, healthcare access, banking, property rental, driving licence conversion, and business setup. We explain real timelines based on current processing speeds, actual costs, and insider knowledge of how Greek bureaucracy actually works—not just how it's supposed to work.

2. Before You Leave Home: Essential Pre-Departure Preparation

Apostilles and Certified Translations for Greek Officials

As with all European moves, apostilles are essential for Greek relocation. An apostille is an official certification that authenticates your documents under the Hague Convention of 1961. Greece recognizes apostilles from all signatory countries, making them mandatory for birth certificates, marriage certificates, educational credentials, and professional qualifications you'll need for Greek residency and employment.

The process is identical to other countries: contact the vital records office in your state or country of birth, pay the apostille fee (typically $10–30), and request the apostille on your birth certificate. Process time is 1–4 weeks depending on the jurisdiction; many offer expedited processing for a fee. Once you have the apostille, you'll need a certified Greek translation. This must be done by a certified translator (μεταφραστής καταρτίσεως—metafrasti katartisis) registered with the Greek courts (Δικαστήριο). Certified translations in Greece cost €30–100 per page depending on document complexity. You can often arrange translations through Greek consulates, professional translation services, or lawyers before arrival, or remotely by sending documents to Greek translation services.

Essential Documents to Prepare

Passport: Must be valid for at least 6 months beyond your intended residency start date. If expiring soon, renew before moving. You'll need it for residence permit application, bank account opening, and all official transactions.

Birth Certificate: Obtain the original or certified copy with apostille. Have it translated into Greek by a certified translator. Keep multiple copies—you'll need it for residence registration, AFM registration, healthcare enrollment, and possibly other legal matters.

Marriage Certificate (if applicable): If married, bring the marriage certificate apostilled and translated. If divorced or widowed, bring the divorce decree or death certificate, both apostilled and translated.

Proof of Income and Financial Stability: Greece requires evidence of sufficient funds for residence and self-support. For employees, bring an employment letter from your employer on company letterhead. For self-employed persons, bring business registration documents and proof of income. For retirees or those with passive income, bring pension statements or investment income documentation. For digital nomads, bring documentation of your business entity (contract with clients, business registration, or similar).

Proof of Accommodation: You'll need a lease agreement (συμβόλαιο ενοικίασης), property purchase agreement, or a letter from a property owner confirming your residence. The document must include the complete address, owner's name and contact information, and signature with date.

Health Insurance: EU citizens should request the EHIC (European Health Insurance Card) before moving. Non-EU citizens must show they have health insurance; without it, your residence permit application will likely be rejected.

3. Residence Permits and Municipal Registration in Greece

EU Citizens: Residence Registration

EU citizens have freedom of movement and residence within the EU, including Greece. You don't need a visa or formal residence permit, but you must register your residence with the local municipality (δήμος—dimos) or regional immigration office within 3 months of arrival. Registration is straightforward: visit your local deme office with your passport, proof of accommodation, and sometimes proof of employment or financial means. Processing is typically 1–2 weeks, and you'll receive a registration certificate (ιστορικό εγγραφής στο δημοτολόγιο).

Non-EU Citizens and Digital Nomads: Residence Permits

Non-EU citizens must obtain a residence permit (άδεια διαμονής) before or immediately upon arrival in Greece. Greece offers several residence permit categories: work, self-employment, study, family reunification, investment, and the increasingly popular digital nomad visa (introduced in 2022 for remote workers earning sufficient income from outside Greece).

The residence permit application process begins at the Ministry of Migration (Υπουργείο Μετανάστευσης) or regional migration service. You'll need your passport, completed application form, passport photos, proof of accommodation, proof of income or employment, health insurance documentation, and criminal record check from your home country (apostilled). Processing time is typically 15–30 days. The residence permit is usually granted for 1–2 years and is renewable. Costs vary from €0 (for some family reunification cases) to €200+ (for self-employment permits).

Digital Nomad Visa (New Resident Program)

Greece introduced the digital nomad visa in September 2022, making it exceptionally attractive for remote workers. To qualify, you must earn a minimum monthly income of approximately €2,500 (this threshold changes; verify current requirements) from clients or employers outside Greece and work remotely. You'll apply through the immigration service with proof of employment/income, a clean criminal record, and health insurance. The permit is typically granted for 1 year and is renewable. Non-EU citizens on digital nomad visas enjoy similar residence rights to EU citizens and can access healthcare and banking on favorable terms.

4. Tax Registration and AFM (Αριθμός Φορολογικού Μητρώου)

What is AFM and Why It Matters

AFM (Αριθμός Φορολογικού Μητρώου—Arithmos Forologikou Mitroou) is your Greek tax identification number, functionally similar to Italy's Codice Fiscale or the US Social Security Number for tax purposes. Every resident and anyone earning income in Greece must have an AFM. You cannot open a bank account, sign an employment contract, register property, or operate a business without it. Your AFM is a unique 10-digit number assigned by the Greek tax authority (ΔΟΣ—Directorate of Social Contributions).

Obtaining Your AFM

To obtain your AFM, visit the local tax office (Δημόσια Υπηρεσία Ρυθμίσεως—DOU—Directorate of Tax Offices) in your municipality or region. Bring your passport, residency certificate from your municipality, and your proof of address (utility bill, lease, or property deed). The tax office will issue your AFM immediately or within 1–2 days. You'll receive a certificate (Ένδειξη Αριθμού Φορολογικού Μητρώου) showing your AFM number. Take a photo of this certificate or request multiple copies—you'll need it when opening bank accounts and conducting official business.

Tax Obligations Based on Residency Status

Once you have an AFM, you're subject to Greek tax law. Greece taxes residents on worldwide income. If you're employed in Greece, your employer withholds taxes (like Italy). If you're self-employed or a digital nomad, you must file quarterly tax returns and pay estimated quarterly taxes. Greece offers special tax regimes for digital nomads and new residents—some pay reduced tax rates (7%) on foreign-source income for the first 7 years, creating tax incentives for remote workers. Consult a Greek tax accountant (λογιστής—logistis) to understand your specific obligations and opportunities.

5. Social Security Registration (AMKA) and Healthcare Access

AMKA: Your Social Security Number

AMKA (Αριθμός Μητρώου Κοινωνικής Ασφάλειας—Arithmos Mitroou Koinonikis Asfaleias) is your social security identification number in Greece, obtained from the local EFKA office (unified social security authority). Like AFM, AMKA is essential for healthcare access, employment, and official transactions. To obtain AMKA, visit your local EFKA office (often called ΚΕΠ—Citizens' Services Center) with your passport, residency certificate, and AFM. Processing is typically same-day or within a few days. You'll receive an AMKA certificate which you should photograph or obtain copies of.

Healthcare Enrollment Through Social Security

Once you have your AMKA, you're automatically enrolled in the Greek National Health Service (Εθνική Σύστημα Υγείας—ESY) if you're employed or paying social security contributions. Greece provides public healthcare to residents, funded through contributions and taxes. As an employed person, you'll have contributions deducted from your salary (approximately 8% employee contribution plus employer contributions). As self-employed, you'll pay contributions (approximately 18–20% of income) directly to EFKA.

6. Opening a Bank Account in Greece

You need a Greek bank account for salary deposits, rent payments, and financial transactions. Major Greek banks include Alpha Bank, Eurobank, National Bank of Greece (NBG), and Piraeus Bank. You'll also find Wise, Revolut, and other fintech options. To open an account, bring your passport, AFM, residency certificate, and proof of income. Processing takes 2–5 business days; some banks offer online opening with video verification for international customers.

Monthly account fees range from €0–15; some banks waive fees if you maintain minimum balances or have regular deposits. International transfer fees vary but are typically €10–25 per transfer from traditional banks; Wise and similar services offer lower rates.

7. Healthcare System and Registration

Greece's public healthcare system (ESY) is available to residents through EFKA registration. You select a primary care doctor (γενικός ιατρός) at your local health center (κέντρο υγείας) and receive referrals to specialists from there. Public healthcare is free or nearly free to users. Many expats also carry private health insurance for faster specialist access and dental care. Private insurance costs €300–1,500 annually depending on age and coverage.

8. Property Rental and Residential Documentation

Most expats rent initially in Greece. Rental agreements must be registered with municipal authorities. Your landlord should provide a lease (συμβόλαιο ενοικίασης) signed by both parties. Key terms include rent amount, payment method, lease duration (typically 1–2 years, renewable), and conditions. Deposits are typically 1–2 months of rent. Once signed, the lease must be registered with the tax authority within 10 days; registration costs €50–100. Ensure your landlord completes this—operating an unregistered lease is illegal and can result in fines.

Your lease serves as your proof of accommodation for residency, banking, and tax registration. Keep a copy signed by both parties and the landlord.

9. Driving Licence Conversion and Transportation

EU driving licences are valid in Greece. You can drive on your EU licence for the duration of your residency. Non-EU licences are valid for 12 months; you must then pass the Greek driving test (in Greek) to obtain a Greek licence, or leave Greece and return to use your licence again. To exchange an EU licence for a Greek one, visit the local Traffic Police office (Τμήμα Τροχαίας) with your licence, residency certificate, AFM, and passport. Exchange is usually free and takes 1–2 weeks.

10. Business Registration and Self-Employment in Greece

If you're self-employed or operating a business in Greece, you must register as an independent professional (ελεύθερος επαγγελματίας—eleutheros epaggelmatias) with the tax authority and social security authority. Visit the local tax office and EFKA with your AFM, business description, and proof of address. You'll receive an economic activity code (ΚΑΕ—KAE). Self-employed contributions to social security are approximately 18–20% of projected income. You'll pay quarterly estimated taxes and file annual returns. Keep meticulous records of all income and expenses.
